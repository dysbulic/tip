Ok, I am home once again. I have been completely removed from Tech's server, so many things are broken and you ought to consider me still incommunicado for another few days still. I am not even sure that this will go out...

The trip went really well though. I really enjoyed the things I got to see and the two volunteers from Senegal that Marc and I were traveling with turned out to be excellent traveling companions.

Unfortunately the trip was not really good for the exhaustion that was already settling in before I left. I am pretty much completely wiped out and the little bit of energy I've got is going to trying to get caught up and getting back online.

So, I will not be particularly gregarious for a bit, but I do have a couple factual bits that are interesting. This first one is just our itenerary...

Day Number Start City End City Travel Time Transport Cost
Day Zero: Nouakchott

The girls came in from Senegal and spent some time visiting places of interest in Nouakchott including the markets to buy silverwork and mulafas, and the hookah bar to smoke a little flavored tobacco.

Day One: Nouakchott Tergit 4.5hr 22kum/4 = 5.5kum

Our first night out was in a little oasis about 30km outside of Atar. It is very likely the most beautiful place in this country. Nestled in the mouth of a canyon with dark orange sand for a carpet and huge palm trees for a ceiling there is a little brook running through that is gathered in one place in a little swimming hole where we took a dip. We arrived in time to climb up to the top of the canyon and watch the sun set over the valley from the top of a 150 meter bluff. Like sitting in a postcard...

Our camping was 1.5kum for the night, 1.5kum for dinner of couscous with chicken, and 600um for breakfast of bread and jam.

We bought out the car and paid to go straight to Tergit. The regular price would be 3kum to Atar in a car with 6 people and then 1kum back to Tergit.

Day Two: Tergit Atar 45min 1.5kum

We spent the night with the volunteers in Atar. It was Easter and I was ashamed when I got my third call from the States from Sharon and had to admit that I'd left my much coveted Easter egg decorating supplies behind because I didn't think I would be able to do them. We did do some very fine deviled eggs though with garlic and dill picked carrots done by the Atar volunteers. We also did dates and creme which is a traditional dish and very tasty.

Room was free. We did 14 eggs which were 700um. The mustard, mayonnaise and creme were each 300um. For dinner we went to a Malian place and had maffe for 200um. The car to Atar would supposedly have been 1kum if we caught it at 6am.

Day Three: Atar Chinguetti 2.5hr 1.3kum

We headed to the ancient city of Chinguetti for our third day. Chinguetti is the seventh holiest city in Islam by virtue of being the seventh oldest. It was once a major stop on the trade routes as well as being a major center of learning with a university and several Islamic libraries. We traveled sitting on top of all of our luggage in the back of a pickup truck. We were the only ones back there though and it wasn't too uncomfortable. Sunscreen is very important though. I was wearing my houlie (think turban) with my mouth covered but my nose exposed and it got quite well toasted while the rest of my face maintained its usual pallid tone.

This is currently the height of the tourist season. I saw many more white faces that I normally see even in Nouakchott. Supposedly it tapers off in May. As we we going to Chinguetti we passed a big cushy tour bus with "Club Adventure" painted on the side. As I shifted my weight and dug my fingers a bit deeper into the netting to keep from rolling off the back of the truck I smiled under my turban at the thought of looking at Mauritania through a window as adventure.

Dinner in Chinguetti was spaghetti and it was about 500um apiece after factoring in some garlic and onions and tomatoes and other bits and pieces.

Day Four: Chinguetti Lagaula 3hr 10kum/4 = 2.5kum

While in Atar we picked up a dune board from a friend of a volunteer. We packed up our board and some supplies and took a camel trek out to an oasis about 12km south of Chinguetti called Lagaula. We headed out at 8 in the morning and arrived a bit before noon. It was too hot to go out so we played some cards while we waited for the sun to set a bit. The evening was passed dune boarding and admiring the spectacular scenery. Boarding was fun. The two disadvantages over snow boarding in my opinion are: one, climbing a dune is some serious work. We would have done well to bring some rope. Two, doing a face plant into sand is something akin to doing it into gravel. It both hurts more and is much less pleasant to have a mouthful of.

Our night in Lagaula was, in a word, cold. We were in a little grass hut with nothing but a plastic mat between us and the ground. I had my houlie pulled over me, but the fabric is really thin. The girls shared a blanket, but I don't think anyone was really cozy.

We bought all of the food that we brought out. A bag of couscous and two of vermicelli. Also some garlic, onions and chicken as well as the obligatory Jumbo™ beef bullion cubes that are present in most everything. We also brought tea, milk and bread. Total cost was probably 1kum apiece for foodstuffs. One of our guides also made us hubsah, a traditional unleavened Mauritanian bread made under the coals of the fire in the sand.

Day Five: Lagaula Chinguetti 3hr already paid

We rented three camels (2kum/camel) with two guides (2kum/guide) and going out one camel carried our stuff while the other two were ridden. Coming back we were moving too slow apparently because our guides doubled us up on the camels. Marc and I had the industrious guide who managed to put a good half a kilometer between us and the girls by the end of the trip. I'd ridden a camel before and this time was much more comfortable, I think because it was on deep sand.

Once we were back in Chinguetti we hid from the sun for most of the day and napped til dinner. We did have bissap (hibiscus) jam which one of the volunteers made and was very tasty.

Dinner was at a local auberge and was 1.5kum for couscous and meat sauce. We treated our hostesses, or course, which added another 1kum.

Day Six: Chinguetti Atar 8hr 1.4kum

This was an interesting day. After visiting one of the libraries (500um) we set out in the morning with the intention of going to Oudane. There are no cars for Oudane from Chinguetti even though the crossroads for Oudane is only about 20km outside of town. Normal operation would be either to rent a car (20kum+) or to go all the way back to Atar (2.5hr). We decided instead to hitch and catch the car which would likely be coming from Atar to Oudane in the evening. We considered the crossroads, but there is no shelter there and it is possible that the car wouldn't stop to get us. Chinguetti is on a plateau and there is a gendarme (state military) stop at the top of the pass. It is there we decided to wait since we would have shelter and the cars have to stop for the gendarmes.

Once there we met a friendly Senegalese fellow who manned the post. He enjoyed very much having some company and in particular the Senegalese volunteer who spoke Wolof. He made us some spaghetti with camel meat and after a while we started playing cards with him. Though he'd never played our game before he proceeded to take a quick lead which entertained him quite a bit. I think our company was a bit too addictive because eventually he stopped inquiring as to the destination of cars and simply quickly waved them through from the door. It was kinda cute, if a bit disquieting. Eventually we decided it was getting too late and so we decided to just go to Atar. One of the Senegalese girls ran out and flagged down a car and we were on our way.

In retrospect, Oudane would have been a better first destination because catching a car at the crossroads going to Chinguetti would have been no problem.

In Atar we did the Malian restaurant again. This time with some beans (200um) and a ginger drink (30um.) For breakfast the next morning we treated our hostesses to cornflakes (1kum/box) and milk (500um/liter.)

Day Seven: Atar Choum 3hr 16kum/4 = 4kum

We headed out around noon to go to Choum. It was a Friday though and everything was closed until 3:00 after the prayer. We needed to get to Choum by 6:00 to catch the train so this looked to be something of a problem. The trip is normally about 1.5kum but we ended up buying out a 4x4 for 16kum so that we could leave right at 3:00. Our car was the Land Rover that time forgot. Apparently from the early '70's Marc theorized it must have served a tour in Vietnam to get as badly beaten as it was. It got us there on time though.

What we later learned is that there is apparently a later train that leaves around 10pm that we could have taken. The ride was over open desert much of the time though, so having space to bounce around was nice. We even picked up a soldier from one of the police stops who rode on the foldout chair set up in the back.

We got some sandwiches as we waited at the garage for three hours. Like most places they were 200um apiece.

Day Seven: Choum Nouadhibou 12hr 0um

A lesson we learned about the iron train. The front cars are ground iron ore which are pretty finely powdered and supposedly soft. The middle cars are crushed red rock varying from gravel sized to fist sized. The final two or three cars (out or perhaps 70-100) are passenger cars. When dropped off the driver will assume you are getting in the passenger cars and drop you where the passenger cars will stop. Once you learn this and take off running to get to the softer iron ore you will not have time to make it and will end up getting stuck on the crushed rock of you don't miss the train entirely.

Another lesson is that freezing cold experienced while lying on crushed rock for 12 hours does not produce the blessed relief of numbness. Rather it generally brings agonizing pain interspersed with bouts of unconsciousness that could be confused by an outside observer with sleep, but which are in fact the body trying to will itself to death to escape the pain and the cold.

Definitely take a many layers of clothing that you do not mind having ruined. Definitely wait in town and get on the front of the train.

Riding in the passenger cars has been likened to sardines and the two people I know of who paid 1000um to do it were pick pocketed. There is supposedly a luxury car for 2.5kum, but I never saw it. I'd personally go with more clothes and ride on top.

Day Eight: Nouadhibou

We hung around Nouadhibou for a day. The girls went to a hamam which is sort of like a steam bath where you get rubbed down and exfoliated. They highly recommended it after coming off the train with iron ground into every pore. There are separate times for men and women (since it is done in the nude) and Marc didn't feel like going, so I didn't either. Instead we visited the port and saw lots of different kinds of fish come rolling in. One of the more interesting was a huge cart loaded down with nothing but sharks.

One of the Nouadhibou volunteers fed us very well during the day. I ate a good pound of brownies and cookies I think. It was very nice. For dinner we went to a Chinese place and had a grilled seafood platter (1.5kum.) It came with these very large and surprisingly tender pieces of octopus as well as shrimp and squid. I practiced my drinking with a beer (500um), but ended up giving half of it to Marc.

Day Nine: Nouadhibou Nouakchott 1hr 10kum

In the morning we walked across the peninsula where Nouadhibou is situated from the bay side to the Atlantic side. It was about 5km and took probably an hour to walk. I've never been to the Atlantic seaboard with the jagged rocks and crashing waves, but it looked like pictures I've seen of that. It had the added excitement of this land was disputed in the '70's between the Polisario and Mauritania and they laid land mines in some places. It wasn't likely would find one, but I've never been in a place where just walking around could get me killed before.

One of the girls wanted to get back home before her birthday, so we decided to fly back to Nouakchott. The airline CMTA has a special rate for volunteers, so our tickets were 10kum and it took an hour for the flight. Going by car would have been at best 8-10 hours and would have probably cost about 6kum. Not a bad deal really.

We flew over the Banc d'Arguin as we went down and it looked really interesting. It is supposedly one of the best bird parks in the world because of the rich fishing waters here and has been a Unesco World Heritage Site since 1989. Supposedly renting a car is about 25kum a day with a driver and I'm seriously thinking about giving that place a visit before I leave.

So, all told I think I spent about 35kum and I was not economizing at all. $100 isn't bad for a tour that I think costs the French tourists in excess of 1000€. I was surprised to learn that the Mauritanian visa was about $160. Apparently the U.S. just doubled the price of their visa and Mauritania responded in kind.
