Mauriwhere?

Peace Corps... For most of my life the term "Peace Corps" conjured to mind images of tanned hippies with long hair and dirty clothes handing out bowls of rice to wide-eyed children with distended bellies. I knew it still existed, but thought of it as something for those youthful idealists committed to saving the world. As my extensive undergraduate career was coming to am close I discovered it could also be an attractive choice to those a little less idealistic but with no real idea of what they want to do save that it wasn't another seven years in college or jumping right into the rat race. I figured that I needed some time to figure out where I was headed and if I could experience some of the world and maybe do a little good in the interim, all the better. I thereby began the year and a half long process that culminated in my walking off a plane in the Islamic Republic of Mauritania last June.

When I first got my invitation my response was "Mauriwhere?" It turned out that Mauritania is actually a pretty big place. In west Africa on the coast below Morocco, at over a million square kilometers it is ten times the size of Tennessee. At the same time it is slowly being consumed by the Sahara desert so less than three million people live here (about half as many as Nashville). The name means "land of the Maures" where the Maures were the nomads who lived here long before the Europeans arrived and started drawing borders. As well as the Maures there are a variety of African tribes represented here as well; particularly the Pulaar, Wolof and Soninke.

At this point I have been a volunteer for about six of the twenty-seven months that I will eventually serve. This country is hot, dirty, poor and inefficient. Accomplishing anything takes monumental effort and Sisyphean patience. At the same time I don't believe that I can describe how much I enjoy being here.

The term "developing world" generally has the sense of a place being incomplete somehow; unfinished. It also means though that this is a country still fledgling and going through processes of development that people in the developed world don't get to experience. The capital city of Nouakchott was founded when the country gained its independence from the French in 1960. It has gone from being a random spot in the sand to being home to over a million people (40-50% of the population) in less time than my dad has been alive. As the Sahara continues its heedless expansion toward the sea many people have been forced here from traditional jobs like herding and farming. You can walk the streets and see the play of how nomadic lifestyles that existed for thousands of years are colliding with modern urbanization.

At this point in my Peace Corps experience I feel like a person who had a completed puzzled knocked from their hands only to discover as they collect the pieces that the picture has changed. It is a bit hard for me to tell you about the picture when I haven't quite gotten it put together myself. So at this point I'll just give you a few highlights of differences from six months ago:

  • I can now pretend to speak French. (My students wish that I could pretend a little better.)
  • I can now tell what time it is by the prayer call being played at the mosque.
  • I no longer need toilet paper, or a toilet for that matter. (What are called "Arab toilets" here we called "a hole in the ground" back home.)
  • I've read Heller, Dostoevsky, Kafka, Faulkner, Gandhi, Solzhenitsyn and about a dozen other good authors. (No TV and the expressive ability of a three year old in French leaves one with much free time.)
  • I can greet (and profane) in half a dozen languages.
  • I find 75 degrees quite balmy and don't start to really get hot till it passes 100.
  • I have ridden a camel, seen my dinner killed and been unable to buy brown sugar because the one store in the country that sells it was closed.
  • I've visited a country other than America. (This was my first.)

In closing I would just like to say to anyone who is looking for a good way to pass two years and who isn't too attached to Wal-Mart and Taco Bell consider giving the Peace Crops a try. It hasn't yet ceased to amaze me.

Will Holcomb graduated without honors in Computer Science in 2002. He is currently an Information and Communications Technology Volunteer serving in the Islamic Republic of Mauritania.
