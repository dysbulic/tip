Zoo

Ben Richey

If anyone is interested in an economical and enjoyable outing in Nouakchott, try visiting the Nouakchott Zoo. It has wisely been placed fairly far away from the city center, so a taxi-ride will be necessary. As most taxi drivers will have no idea what you're talking about if you ask for the zoo, try saying, "Carrefour Madrid", getting out and walking south, and then asking all the kids that you see, "Mneyn bleyda bil yassir min haywanaat?"

Eventually you will be directed to a metal grating in a wall with a man behind it that may or may not be asleep. If he is asleep, congratulations, you get in for free. If he is awake, you will have to pay the one-hundred ouguiya entrance fee, but will get a nice memento ticket-stub that you can carry in your billfold for three or four days afterward. The actually door to the zoo is around a corner and just in front of a family tent that may or may not be associated with the zoo, (there will be an old toothless woman cackling and pointing into the open entranceway, she may or may not be an official usher. She might just be crazy.)

The first thing that strikes you are the wonderful smells associated with caging large animals. The flies that will have formed a black mask across the nearby lion's face will come and greet you, trying to make a similar mask upon your face. Don't mind thenn just wave them away. They will quickly enough understand and return to the more-festering face of the lion. The lioness may or may not be dead when you get there, if so, you'll probably enjoy the male twice as much. Try to make him roar by poking at him with one of the sticks lying nearby. It really is an impressive sound.

In fine Nouakchott fashion, Nature's greatest enemies have been placed directly next to each other; beside the lions are the hyenas. Kind of like the miniature poodles of America, these mini-hyenas are less of the bone-crunching variety and more of the lying apathetically in the corner type. But they do have sweet, puppy-dog eyes.

If you can manage to tear yourself away from the hyenas, there are two nearby cages with monkeys and baboons. A woman sits near the cages selling bananas and cookies to feed the monkeys and baboons, but cigarettes also work. The baboons will begin to eat the cigarette, decide not to, and then attempt to stick it in a neighboring baboon's anus. Don't make the mistake of sitting too close to the bars while taunting the baby baboon. (Otherwise you might leave having your tee-shirt sleeve ripped open, or the tip of your finger left bleeding. The nearby woman's chant of "huwa hatiir, huwa hatiir" should be believed.)

After the primates, go see the crocodile, it may or may not be real. Either way, it is fun to throw pebbles at. Then there is a bird that may or may not be a stork, three turkeys, and a duck. The duck is especially exciting. You can sit on the concrete ledge outside the cage and watch his antics for hours on end. If you continue around the loop that the succession of cages makes, you'll next pass a wall with paintings of giraffes and leopards on it. In absence of the real animals, the paintings are quite satisfactory. The leopard especially is in a strikingly realistic pose of attack, and the black dots are painted with amazing skill. You might even be inspired to go back to your home and paint a leopard of your own.

After the painted animals, there are two empty cages, (one of them has a vulture in it, but it may be there of its own accord.) Then again the lions. You can make a second trip around the cages if you are in that kind of mood, if not you should leave by the exit you came in through. If you are really lucky, there might be a man out in the parking lot with an air-rifle who will bet you twenty ouguiya that you can't shoot the Fanta can sitting ten feet away in the sand. If you are a good shot, it is an easy way to win back the one-hundred you had to pay to enter the zoo. When you get home, be sure to wash off any blood that may or may not be yours. And then tell all your friends about how you went to the zoo.
