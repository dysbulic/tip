8 Levels of Development In Mentor

  • Level 0: Limbo

    • There’s something going on, but I don’t want to do the work; student may know what to do, but is scared

  • Level 1: Introduction

    • Read basic materials: growth chapter, values, critical thinking, MBTI, Perry/Fowler/Kohlberg, bond with Connie, journaling

  • Level 2: Simple Groups

  • Level 3: Complex Groups

  • Level 4: Leading Small Groups (committees, club offices, taking authenticity into external environment)

  • Level 5: Advance Leadership and more complex materials

  • Level 6: Dyad work with Connie

    • Includes assisting with students at any previous level

  • Level 7: undefined

  • Level 8: undefined

Tracks In Mentor

  • Track 1: Personal (INF)

    • Who are you?

    • What do you want?

    • “I” journaling and affirmation from teacher

    • Perry 4A—lose the mask

    • Autonomy and reception

  • Track 2: Interpersonal (ENF)

    • Group work

  • Track 3: Subject area (SJ)

    • Career/major

    • E.g., English majors doing pre-grad-school preparation as career mentoring

  • Track 4: Objective / Intellectual (NT)

    • E.g., Gödel, Escher, Bach, critical thinking, Disturbing The Universe, Einstein’s Ideas And Opinions, enlarging your own ideas

  • Track 5: World cultures / religions / multicultralism

  • Track 6: Basic Spirituality (as individual); some covered in Track 1, but Track 6 grows out of Track 5

  • Track 7: Wisdom

  • Track 8: Mysticism

General Notes

  • Models of mentoring

    • Wisdom comes from Mentor

    • Wisdom comes from student

    • Wisdom comes from student and is enhanced by group

    • Cf. Browning: “God uses us to help each other so, lending our minds out.” —Fra Lippo Lippi)

  • Video tape typical mentor meeting?

  • Openness vs. confidentiality: name them, frame them, fame them

  • Mentor List

    • Begun by Frank Fujita to try to continue mentoring environment.

    • Originally very structured: a book a month, movie bi-weekly

    • Frank eventually gives up on this and opens it to general discussion

  • Things evolve: don’t stay attached to your original structure

  • 

  • Younger students are helped by seeing former/older students: they introject maturity, but also see them as less advanced, see them continuing the workd

  • Pain and struggle: working on one issue pulls in other things “stuck to it.” When it explodes, it’s like chewing gum stuck on it; something that doesn’t belong seems to be hooked in

  • Secret program vs. open program

  • Sofas made the difference

  • Learning by doing vs. learning from books: you need the principles and the vocabulary, but you need to do it, too

  • Many students don’t want to “get deep”

  • What does the student think he’s doing, vs. what Connie thinks he’s doing?

  • Sufi model works best: work with what’s there. It’s all play, all learning

  • Card-carrying mystic: strongly S, strongly N, then both integrative

  • Mentor can get kids to work on Mentor’s pet topics, but also work on what the kids are interested in

  • Down side to letting kids see Mentor learn/fumble: can scare off students at Level 0 and Level 1, in whom it can create a lack of confidence in the Mentor and impede the “bonding” process

  • Service learning

  • “Levels” of Mentor are not age-dependent; they are rather work-dependent and focus-dependent
