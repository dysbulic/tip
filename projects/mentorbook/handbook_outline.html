  1. Volume One: Learning Mentor

    1. Introduction to Mentor
      1. Goals and Program Structure
        1. The Role of the Teacher
        2. The Role of the Student
        3. How Connie’s program works
      2. The Mentor Path
      3. Mentor Values, Community, and the Individual
        1. Confidentiality
        2. Community Support
        3. Acceptance & Unconditionality
        4. 14 Precepts (Thich Naht Hahn)
        5. Why alcohol and drugs aren’t tolerated (and don’t solve life’s problems!)
      4. Why Be in Mentor?
      5. Personality Autonomy, Participation, & Outcomes

      Notes: In this section of the handbook, a basic outline of the mentor program is provided. The first section explains the mentor program and provides a set of guidelines (e.g., no drugs, concepts of confidentiality, active participation in activities and meetings, etc.) for working in mentor. The second section provides an explanation of the unique mentor path that evolves for those who participate in the program. The third section looks at important ideas and values of those who participate in mentor, the sohbet of mentor, and the individual’s role in mentor. The fourth section addresses the question "why be in mentor?" by exploring the benefits in growth, fellowship, and understanding that can result from participating in the program. The fifth section examines the issue of autonomy relating to choices that the student makes while participating in the mentor program, the nature of participation and involvement in the program, and assesses potential effects of participating in mentor.

The section addresses the meaning of the phrase and its connotations when implemented in the individual’s life, the benefits of self-knowledge, and how self-knowledge and understanding can be achieved in daily life. The section ends with an autobiography of Dr. Hood’s personal life and how self-realization and personal understanding were created.

      Notes: In this section of the handbook, psychological topics relevant to the mentor student are explored in detail. The section begins with a study of the psychology of growth and development and discusses prominent models (Perry, Fowler, and Kohlberg) relating to the development of the individual. Depression, suicide, and Perry five issues, topics which pose great difficulty to many mentor students, are examined. Through the work of Carol Pierson, psychological archetypes are explored. The MBTI is explored in detail. Psychology relating to values and worldviews is also explored through Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs, Rogers’ Four Axioms, and Campbell’s exploration of the life journey. The final section deals with the relationship between happiness and the mentor path . This chapter serves the student in better understanding the mentor path in light of modern psychology.

    4. Clarity & Critical Thinking
      1. Clarity
        1. AWARENESS
        2. Empathy
        3. Mindtalk
        4. Co-dependency
        5. Rumi — "The Clear Bead"
      2. Critical Thinking
        1. The art of thinking clearly
        2. Logical Fallacies
      3. Assimilation
        1. The Sisyphus Effect
        2. Paradox
        3. Ambiguity
        4. Uncertainty

      Notes: In this section of the handbook, critical thinking, learning clarity in thought and belief, and assimilation of worldviews is covered. The first section attempts to help the mentor student learn to see other points of view, eliminate self-defeating beliefs, and learn how to not use rose-colored glasses when viewing the surrounding world. The next section works on critical thinking . The final section works on the assimilation of many and contrasting views encountered by the student.

    5. Interpersonal Intelligence
      1. What is Interpersonal Intelligence?
      2. Empathy
      3. Love
      4. Understanding
      5. How Interpersonal Intelligence Can Solve Many of Life’s Problems
      6. Using interpersonal intelligence in daily life

      Notes: Among the many types of intelligence that human beings possess, interpersonal intelligence is an important type of understanding that is often downplayed and overlooked. Yet, an understanding of others through empathy and psychological tools is often not practiced, resulting in much conflict and frustration that can be avoided by acknowledging and appreciating differences in the world. This section of the mentor handbook explores the concept of interpersonal intelligence and how it can be developed and used to solve many problems when interacting with others at work, in relationships, and in dealing with others. The topics of love, understanding, and empathy & their relationship to interpersonal intelligence are also explored. The section ends with a discussion on how interpersonal intelligence can be developed and used in daily life.

    6. Activities

        1. The Importance of Activities & the Sohbet Community
        2. Life-Training, SEE, & POP
        3. Progoff
        4. Other
      2. Micro
        1. Meditation
        2. Journaling
        3. Transformation

      Notes: Aside from books, movies, plays, etc. that benefit the mentor student, participation in mentor activities is important for the students own personal development. Activities, divided into subjects at the macro or interpersonal level and the micro or personal level, are examined. At the macro level, activities such as mentor meetings, Life-Training, SEE, Progoff, and other activities are discussed. At the micro level activities, such as meditation, journaling, and the process of personal transformation (through goal implementation, therapy, etc.) are examined.

    7. Books, Movies, and Other Good Stuff
      1. Connie’s Essential Reading List
      2. Connie’s Essential Movie List
      3. Other things Dr. C Recommends

      Notes: A list of various books, movies, and other things that Connie finds extremely beneficial to complete while in mentor. These lists are arranged at various levels to provide maximum benefit for mentor students at various places along the mentor path.

  2. Volume Two: Worldviews, Life Paths & the Process of Becoming

    1. Letting Go — Flying and Seeing
      1. Learning to See Issues and Move Beyond Impediments to Growth
      2. Common Issues
        1. Issues of Faith
        2. Personal Attachments
        3. Irrational Statements
        4. Relationship issues
          1. Family
          2. Significant Other
          3. Group Think
      3. Defenses
        1. Freud
        2. Common Defenses
      4. The Existential Crisis
      5. Why move beyond personal issues?

      Notes: In this section of the handbook, the student to recognize personal issues and move beyond impediments to growth so that greater health may be obtained. The first section covers learning how to see personal issues and moving past these issues. The second section covers common issues that students encounter. The third section covers psychological theories of defenses and notes common defenses/games that people play to avoid dealing with issues. The fourth section covers common problems and the existential crises that come when dealing with personal issues. The final section examines the benefits of moving beyond issues (i.e., letting go) and dealing with problems encountered in daily life.

    2. Intellectual Ideas

      Notes: In this section of the mentor handbook, many prominent intellectual ideas are explored from philosophy, social sciences, mathematics, English, and other subjects. Through exploration of the writings of J.S. Mill, Russell’s paradox, Heigel, and others, the mentor student can gain a broader understanding of the diversity of thought and opinion in the surrounding world. The article also examines the works of Sacks and Hoftstadter in their exploration of modern congnition.

    3. Religions and Other Cultures
      1. Metapatterns & Ethnocentrism
      2. Christianity
      3. Buddhism
      4. Hinduism
      5. Islam
      6. Cultures

      Notes: In this section of the handbook, major world religions and cultures are discussed. The exploration provides specific information on various religious and cultural practices to broaden the mentor student’s understandings of the diversity in belief and practice that the world contains. Metapatterns and ethnocentrism are also discussed to provide the student with a better view of the human components of religion and the prevalence of the "I’m right, others views and practices are wrong" mentality that is often experienced in the world today.

    4. Wisdom Path
      1. Wisdom & the Structure of the Wisdom Path
      2. The Characteristics and Benefits of Wisdom
      3. Wisdom Quotes

      Notes: In this chapter, wisdom, the structure of the wisdom path (as taught by various religious and intellectual traditions), and the significance of possessing wisdom are explored.

    5. Mystical Path
      1. Mysticism and the Mystical Path
      2. Christian Mystics
      3. Buddhist & Hindu Mystics
      4. Those Crazy Sufis

      Notes: In this section of the handbook, the mystical path is explored. Prominent mystics such as the Sufis, Rumi, Buddha, John of the Cross are studied along with critical texts such as Underhill.

    6. The Good Life
      1. Before & After Mentor
      2. Freedom to Choose
      3. The Art of Becoming
      4. Tying it all together

      Notes: In this final section of the handbook, a look at mentor and its potential affects on students is provided. A look at previous students following the mentor path is first provided. The benefits of the good life and the process of achieving the good life are then discussed. Finally, the handbook provides an overview of the mentor structure and how to use the book for greatest benefit.
