****CHARACTERISTIC OF WISDOM

Part II: Approaches informed by philosophical conceptions of wisdom

2.  Wisdom through the ages -- Daniel N. Robinson
(History of wisdom)
A. Plato:  a.sophia -- philosopher's knowledge, devotion to pursuit of truth
            b. phronesis  -- practical knowledge, locates prudent actions,
resists passions
            c. episteme --scientific knowledge, the nature of things and
the principles governing behavior

B. Aristotle: "character" revealed by "deliberated choices and
dispositions," rightly disposed passions,                strives for moral
perfection/virtue

C. Stoic & Epicurean: seek secure serenity, knows how to achieve
minimization of suffering, fully reconciling oneself to governed events of
cosmos; acceptance w/o knowing why
D. Christian: a state of being, to be touched by Divine wisdom conveying

        timeless and boundless verities
E. Scientism & Romanticism: Divine gift, discovered through introspective

        processes & manifested in words/works of beauty, without empiricism


3. The psychology of wisdom: an evolutionary interpretation --Mihaly
Csikszentmihalyi and Kevin Rathunde
(3 types of wisdom)
A. Cognitive process
  a. deals with enduring universal truths not fleeting phenomenon
  b. attempt to find how various aspects of reality are related to each
other -- unspecialized
  c. implies a hierarchical ordering of truths & action directed at those truths

B. Virtue -- socially valued behavior pattern
  a.  knowledge of the connectedness between cause and effect determines
course of action
  b.  basis of morality, long term applications for individual and society

C.  Personal Good -- personally desirable state or condition for here and now
  a. needed for attainment of reward:  to get real pleasure from health,
satisfaction from fame,good use of money
  b. contemplation of universal order is intrinsically reveling of self and
universe


4. Wisdom as integrated thought: historical and developmental perspectives --
        Gisela Labouvie-Vief
A. greater awareness of complexity and tolerance of ambiguity
B. acceptance of responsibility for conflict -- sees symmetry of power
C.  subjective and communal are a necessary part of one's endeavors to be
objective
D. combination of free emotional expression with conscious relation leads
to better acceptance of another's separate feelings
E. evolves flexible degree in which conflicts between impulse & norm, self
& society, inner & outer are acknowledged and integrated within a more
autonomously chosen structure
F. ability to obtain an androgynous sexuality, knowledge of inner world
G. moral and spiritual leadership -- generative investment in others
H. mental processes that hold the paradoxical tension of thought being both
immanent and transcendent
I. ability to see through & beyond individual uniqueness & specialness into
those structures relating us in our common humanity



Part III: Approaches informed by folk conceptions of wisdom

5. Toward a psychology of wisdom and it ontogenesis-- Paul B. Baltes and
Jacqui Smith
 (wisdom model)
A. Everyday definition: Good judgment and advice about important but
uncertain matters of life.
B. Theoretical defn: An expert knowledge system in the domain, fundamental
life pragmatics (e.g. life planning, life management, life review)
c. Functional consequence: exceptional insight into human development and
life matters, exceptionally god judgment, advice, and commentary about
difficult life problems.
D. Family of 5 criteria
  a. Rich factual knowledge -- general and specific knowledge about the
conditions of life and its variations
  b. Rich procedural knowledge--general and specific knowledge about
strategies of judgment and advice concerning matters of life
  c. Life span contextualism-- knowledge about the contexts of life and
their temporal (developmental) relationships
  d. Relativism-- knowledge about differences in values, goals, and priorities
  e. Uncertainty--knowledge about the relative indeterminacy and
unpredictability of life and ways to manage


6. Wisdom in a postapocalyptic age -- Michael J. Chandler with Stephen Holliday
A. Knowledge of many different things, not a specialized knowledge --
broader form of human understanding, can cut across particulars arriving at
a view having the widest scope of possible applications
B. ability to peer through gloom of present age & recover preapocalyptic
vision of knowledge types we have forgotten long ago -- ability to see
past, present uses of knowledge, and see different forms in future



7. Wisdom and its relations to intelligence and creativity-- Robert J. Sternberg
(6 components)
A. Reasoning ability-- has the unique ability to look at a problem or
situation and solve it; has good problem-solving ability; has a logical
mind; is good at distinguishing between correct and incorrect answers; is
able to apply knowledge to particular problems; is able to put old
information together in new ways; has a huge store of information; has the
ability to recognize similarities and differences; has rationality (reason
clearly); makes connections and distinctions between ideas and things
B.  Sagacity-- displays concern for others; considers advice; understand
people through dealing with a variety of people; feels he or she can always
learn from other people; knows self best; is thoughtful; is fair; is a god
listener; is not afraid to admit making a mistake, will correct, learn, and
go on; listens to all sides of an issue
C. Learning from ideas and environment-- attaches important to ideas; is
perceptive; learns from other people's mistakes
D. Judgment-- acts within own physical and intellectual limitation; is
sensible; has good judgment at all times; thinks before acting or making
decisions; is able to see long term outcomes; thinks before speaking; is a
clear thinker
E. Expeditious use of information-- is experienced; seeks out information,
especially details; has age, maturity, or long experience; learns and
remembers and gains information from past mistakes of successes; changes
mind on basis of experience
F. Perspicacity-- has intuition; can offer solutions that are on the side
of right and truth; is ale to see through things; has the ability to
understand and interpret his or her environment
G. Sees limitations yet seeks truth & evaluates information to see how it
relates to truth, welcomes ambiguity, and tries to understand phenomenon in
depth


8. The study of wise persons: integrating a personality perspective --
Lucinda Orwoll and Marion Perlmutter
A.  Uncommon levels of self-awareness & psychological growth -- mature and
integrated personalities
B. ability to move beyond individual concern to more collective/universal
issues-- egocentered to universal apprehension of reality
C. perception of understanding with humanitarian and global feelings of concern
D. self-development 7 self-transcendence



Part IV. Approaches informed by psychodevelopmental conceptions of wisdom

9. The loss of wisdom -- John A. Meacham
A. knowing that one does not know because there is no limit to what can be known
B. knowledge is fallible belief -- strives to construct new uncertainties,
doubts, and questions of what might be known
C. adding to knowledge one hold while at same time recognizing there is
much one doesn't know
D. attitude taken by persons toward beliefs, values, knowledge,
information, abilities, & skills that are held, a tendency to doubt that
these are necessarily true or valid and to doubt that they are an
exhaustive set of those things that could
 be known


10. Wisdom and Reflective Judgment: knowing in the face of uncertainty --
Karen Strohm Kitchener and Helene G. Brenner
A. movement beyond universal issues that can be known with certainty
through process of logical/formal reasoning to understanding issues of
knowing in the face of uncertainty
B. an awareness of unknown & implications for real world problem solving
and judgment
C. the presence of unavoidably difficult "thorny" problems inherent in the
lives of adults
D. a comprehensive grasp of knowledge characterized by both breadth and depth
E. a recognition that knowledge is uncertain and that it is not possible
for truth to be absolutely knowable at any given time
F. a willingness and exceptional ability to formulate sound, executable
judgments in the face of this uncertainty
G. intellectual ability allowing few individuals to make particularly
exceptional judgment at uncertain, problematic life issues
H. more than logic & is generally uncalled for in well-structured problems


11. Wisdom: the art of problem finding -- Patricia Kennedy Arlin
A. means by which one discovers, envisages, or goes into deeper questions;
problem finding
B. the search for complementarity (finding similarities between things
previously isolated)
C. the detection of symmetry in the face of that which appears symmetrical
and in equilibrium
D. openness to change: its possibility and its reality
E. a sense  of taste for problems that are of fundamental importance
F. the preference for certain conceptual moves
(see p 237 in the 1990 copyright for a long list of wisdom characteristics
-- too long to type)


12. An essay on wisdom: toward organismic processes that make it possible
-- Juan Pascual-Leone
A. ultimate possible achievement of a normal person's growth
B. category describing moment in development in development when
psychological system fully coordinates across experiential contexts &
alternatives forms/modes of processing
C. anchored in present, has dialectically integrated into a manifold
totality the multiple aspects of his/her self-experience & has done so not
just conceptually but experientially, historically, & culturally
D. appears when dialectical integration reaches sufficient breadth &
cohesion over person's control processes & over his/her storehouse of
informational processes (cognitive, affective, inter/intrapersonal,
religious, existential,cultural, etc.)
E. ability to resolve dialectical contradictions that emerge in certain
contexts, to apply these resolutions to different life contexts in order to
build a more autonomous self
F. appears with emergence of progressively higher levels of
affect/self-control which with advancement brings about a better
dialectical integration of personality
G. a weakening of ego centered characteristics, leading to a greater
intuition & empathic understanding of others, self, world, and nature as
equally strong concerns


13. Conceptualizing wisdom: the primacy of affect-cognition -- Deirdre A. Kramer
A. maintains a certain set of assumptions at social reality & is able to
effectively apply these to a variety of domains in order to resolve
problems arising in his/her own experience, advise others, shape social
institutions, & seek meaning and continuity in experience
B. integration of thoughts and feelings (interdependent cognition and affect)
C. enables individuals to adopt and fosters resolutions to tasks of adult life
(5 functions)
1. enable one to resolve dilemmas & make decisions
2. advising others dilemmas/decisions
3. management and guidance of society
4. life review/evaluation
5. question meaning of life
(precursors to wisdom)
1. recognize individuality
2. seeing context
3. cooperative, empathic strategy for interpersonal interaction
4. recognize possible for change
5. recognize necessity of integrated cognition and affect



Part V. Integration of approaches and viewpoints

14. The elements of wisdom: overview and integration -- James E. Birren and
Laurel M. Fisher

A. multidimensional construct; blending of cognitive, affective, & conative
(volition) elements
B. not dominated by emotions, so maintains reflective state, generating
alternative solutions to problems
C. weighs known & unknowns, resists overwhelming emotion wile maintaining
interest & carefully chooses when & where to take action
