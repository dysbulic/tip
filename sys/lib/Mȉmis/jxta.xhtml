JXTA — Juxtopose

JXTA is a suite of six protocols for establishing a P2P network designed by Sun Microsystems in 2001.

Network Concepts

The fundamental building block of a JXTA network is, reasonably enough, the peer:

Each peer supports services which it offers to the network. Services are exposed through endpoints. At a minimum, peers are required to implement:

  • Endpoint Service — A, perhaps partial, implmentation of the Endpoint Routing Protocol (ERP) used to send and receive messages.
  • Resolver Service — An implementation of the Peer Resolver Protocol (PRP) used for issuing queries and later identifying responses.

Two additional protocols are available for peers with more in-depth needs:

  • Peer Discovery Protocol (PDP) — used by peers to advertise their resources and discover resources from other peers.
  • Peer Information Protocol (PIP) — used to obtain status information from other peers.

Endpoints are connected to one another via pipes and there is support for both point-to-point and propogate (multicast) pipes. The Pipe Binding Protocol (PBP) is used by peers to establish a pipe between one or more endpoints.

In a JXTA network, peers are only connected a small number of the peers in a group. The Rendezvous Protocol (RVP) is used by edge peers to resolve resources, propagate messages, and advertise local resources by communicating with rendezvous peers.

Peers belong to one or more groups. As well as limiting scope, group membership can require authentication and provide data security.
